Urinary Catheterization

Guidelines I – III: CST qualifications, indications for catheter use, sterile technique for insertion, catheter sizing, and balloon selection. Revised April 14, 2017.

🎯
Core principle: To prevent the patient from acquiring a UTI and maintain metabolic homeostasis, the principles of asepsis and sterile technique must be strictly followed when inserting and removing a urinary catheter. UTIs are the second most common healthcare-associated infection, and most are linked to urinary catheterization.

⚠️Risk factors for CAUTI
  • #1 risk factor: prolonged use of an indwelling catheter
  • Method of catheterization
  • Not maintaining a closed drainage system
  • Patient's susceptibility due to weakened immune system
  • High-risk groups: women, elderly, immunocompromised, diabetics, spinal injury patients (hemiplegic, quadriplegic), pregnant women (4–5% develop UTI)
🎓Guideline I — CST qualifications
  • Must be a graduate of a CAAHEP-accredited program or ABHES-accredited program and certified CST with currency
  • CSTs have detailed knowledge of genitourinary anatomy to prevent insertion/removal errors and patient injury
  • Experts in principles of asepsis and sterile technique for catheter insertion, removal, and specimen collection
  • Qualified for catheter insertion, removal, and maintenance per Core Curriculum for Surgical Technology requirements
  • Guideline II: Must complete training in accessory devices (stylet, guide wire, filiforms can cause patient injury) in simulated training sessions before using on patients

Guideline III — Appropriate indications for indwelling catheter use

⚠️
Catheters should only be used when medically necessary — not as part of routine surgical patient care. The use and duration must be minimized, especially in high-risk patients.
✅Appropriate perioperative indications (CDC HICPAC 2009)
  • Lengthy surgical procedure
  • Patients undergoing genitourinary surgery
  • Anticipated need to monitor intraoperative urinary output
  • Anticipated need for large volume of infusions or diuretics during surgery
  • Anticipated postoperative reasons — e.g., prolonged epidural anesthesia recovery, transurethral prostate removal
  • Removal timing: the catheter should be removed within 24 hours postoperatively unless there are specific indications for prolonged use
  • The catheter must be removed as soon as it is no longer needed — minimize duration especially in elderly, immunocompromised patients, and women

Guideline III — Sterile technique for insertion

🧤Sterile catheter kit contents & technique
  • Hand wash performed immediately before and after insertion, removal, or manipulation of the catheter device or insertion site
  • Use a sterile catheter kit containing: sterile gloves (donned using the open gloving technique); fenestrated drape; sponges; antiseptic solution for cleansing the urethral meatus; single-use packet of lubricant jelly; catheter with collection bag
  • Use a preconnected, sealed catheter-tubing junction to maintain a closed system
  • Antimicrobial/antiseptic-impregnated catheters should not be routinely used
  • Routine use of antiseptic lubricant jelly is not necessary
  • Use the smallest diameter catheter that will accomplish drainage unless otherwise ordered — minimizes bladder neck and urethral trauma
  • Secure the catheter tubing after insertion to prevent urethral traction and inadvertent catheter removal (especially when moving patient from OR bed to stretcher)

Catheter sizing & balloon selection

✅Correct balloon sizes
  • Pediatric patients: 1.5 mL or 3 mL balloon
  • Adult patients: 5 mL balloon, filled per manufacturer instructions
  • Pretest the balloon based on manufacturer's recommendations — some manufacturers (e.g., Bard) pretest each Foley catheter before packaging, so pretesting may not be necessary
🚫30 mL balloon — never use
  • When filled, the 30 mL balloon positions itself high in the bladder
  • Places undue pressure on the bladder neck and pelvic floor → bladder spasms and leakage
  • Results in stasis — drainage eyes are positioned superior to the balloon (above the urine)
  • Silicone balloons: pretesting not recommended — silicone can form a cuff or crease at the balloon area that causes urethral trauma during insertion and removal

CAUTI Prevention

Guidelines III – V: catheter maintenance, drainage system integrity, urine specimen collection, device selection, and quality improvement programs.

🎯
Core principle: A sterile, continuously closed drainage system must be maintained at all times. Any break in the system requires immediate replacement using sterile technique. A reminder-based CAUTI reduction system has been shown to reduce CAUTI rates by 53%.

Guideline III — Catheter maintenance rules

Guideline III · Closed System
Maintaining the closed drainage system
▼
  • A sterile, continuously closed drainage system must be maintained at all times to prevent CAUTI
  • If a break in sterile technique, disconnection, or urine leakage occurs: the catheter and collecting bag must be replaced using sterile technique and sterile supplies
  • Unobstructed urine flow must be maintained; backflow of urine from the bag must be prevented
  • Catheter and collecting tube must be kept from kinking and bending
  • The collecting bag must always be kept below the level of the bladder to prevent backflow — and must not be placed on the floor
  • This is especially critical when transferring the patient between the stretcher and OR bed and vice versa
  • No clamp should be placed on the catheter or collecting tube when emptying the collection bag or removing the catheter
Guideline III · Emptying the Bag
Emptying the collecting bag during lengthy procedures
▼
  • During lengthy surgical procedures or prolonged PACU stays, the collecting bag should be emptied using a clean collecting container
  • The CST or HCW must wear PPE: gloves and protective eyewear
  • Avoid splashing; prevent the drainage opening of the collecting bag from contacting the nonsterile collecting container
  • The amount of urine must be recorded in the patient's operative record or PACU record
Guideline III · Urine Specimen
Collecting a sterile urine specimen
▼
  • Urine specimen for culture/urinalysis must be obtained using the needleless sampling port with a sterile syringe/cannula adaptor
  • The port must be cleansed with a disinfectant before accessing
  • Urine must not be obtained from the collection bag for culture — specimen from the bag is not considered sterile

Guideline IV — Catheter device selection process

👥Multidisciplinary evaluation team
  • Team must consist of non-managerial employees who provide direct patient care: CSTs, surgeons, RNs, infection control officer, materials management/purchasing, and risk management
  • Gather data on current device: ease of use, effectiveness, types and frequencies of CAUTIs
  • Selection and priority criteria: patient safety; device performance; ease and efficiency of use; user acceptance
  • Gather information on available products from manufacturers, evidence-based research, and feedback from other surgery departments already using the products; request samples
  • Develop an anonymous, one-page evaluation form with written comment section; establish a time period for evaluation
  • Tabulate and analyze results — the final decision must not be solely based on cost; also consider training costs and potential CAUTI-reduction cost savings
  • After implementation: monitor correct use, gather ongoing feedback, and identify if device needs to be replaced

Guideline V — Quality improvement (QI) program

📊

A systematic review found that using a "urinary catheter reminder" checklist — to identify all patients with catheters and assess the need for continued catheterization — reduced the CAUTI rate by 53%.

📋QI program components
  • Assure appropriate indications for catheter use
  • Identify when catheters are no longer needed
  • Ensure adherence to asepsis and hand hygiene practices
  • Multidisciplinary team: CSTs, surgeons, RNs, infection control officer, risk management
  • System of documentation; continuing education and performance feedback
  • Procedure-specific guidelines for insertion, maintenance, and removal
  • Annual review of policies and procedures
📄Required documentation in patient record
  • Physician order for catheter placement
  • Indications for catheter insertion
  • Date and time of catheter insertion
  • Name and credentials of individual who inserted the catheter
  • Date and time catheter was removed
  • Recorded in a standard format for data collection and QI purposes
  • Electronic documentation that can be easily searched is recommended
📈Measuring QI program success
  • Success measured by decreased CAUTI rates and decreased catheter-days
  • Share QI results with surgery personnel to promote awareness and "buy-in"
  • Track and share: hand hygiene compliance rate, catheter removal rates in PACU, and CAUTI rates in the HDO
  • Collect formal (surveys) and informal (verbal) feedback from surgical personnel on what is working and what can be improved

Sterility Indicators

Guidelines I – III: chemical indicators (all 6 classes), biological indicators, physical monitoring, and quality assurance systems. Revised April 14, 2017.

🎯
Core principle: All items handled by sterile team members and those contacting patient tissues must be sterile to prevent SSI. Guaranteeing sterility requires a quality control system: physical monitoring of sterilization machines, chemical indicators (CI), and biological indicators (BI). Indicators also help pinpoint and resolve processing failures.
⚠️
Critical distinction: Chemical indicators (CI) demonstrate that a package was exposed to specific sterilization processing conditions — they do not prove the enclosed items are sterile. Only biological indicators (BI) provide a direct measure of the lethality of the sterilization process.

Guideline I — Class 1 external CI (Process Indicator)

📦Purpose & placement
  • Affixed to or printed on the outside of every HDO-assembled instrument tray, package, or rigid container
  • Exception: packages that allow visualization of the internal CI (e.g., paper-plastic peel packs with clear plastic side) do not require a separate external CI
  • Types: sterilizer indicator tape, indicating labels, indicating strips, or color-changing printed legends — chemically impregnated
  • Upon exposure to sterilization: visible, even color change (the "endpoint" response)
  • Commercial packages typically include an external color-changing printed legend that must be clearly observable to the CST
  • Must be used per manufacturer's IFU; manufacturers are required to provide written IFU on storage, handling, and use
🔍CST interpretation responsibilities
  • CST must visualize the external CI (or internal CI in peel packs) before opening any package in the OR to confirm sterilization exposure and even color change
  • If the CST's interpretation suggests the package was inadequately exposed to sterilization conditions: do not open the package — return to the Central Sterile Processing Department (CSPD) for reprocessing
  • External CIs help detect potential sterilization failures
  • Part of overall quality assurance program alongside physical monitoring and BIs

Guideline II — Internal CI (Classes 3, 4, 5, or 6)

🔬Internal CI — retrieval and interpretation
  • Required within each HDO-assembled package per ANSI/AAMI ST79
  • Cannot be retrieved without opening the package — must be retrieved at the time the package is to be used
  • Must be retrieved, visualized, and interpreted during preoperative case management (while setting up the sterile back table and Mayo stand)
  • If the CST determines sterilization was inadequate: contents are non-sterile — immediately remove the package from the sterile field
  • CST must determine how much of the sterile field was potentially contaminated:
    • Example 1: rigid container on basin stand not yet placed on back table → back table does not need to be broken down
    • Example 2: sterile linen towels tossed onto back table that may have touched other sterile items → the towels and all touched items are non-sterile; entire affected area must be broken down
  • Hand the non-sterile package to the circulator — return to CSPD with the internal CI and load identification information for reprocessing
  • CST must change gloves — gloves are contaminated from handling non-sterile items

The six classes of chemical indicators

Class 1
Process Indicator
External CI — affixed to outside of HDO-assembled packages. Distinguishes processed from unprocessed. Does not prove sterility.
Class 2
Specific Test Indicator
Used for specific sterilizer tests. Example: the Bowie-Dick air-removal test indicator for pre-vacuum steam sterilizers.
Class 3
Single-Variable Indicator
Reacts to one critical sterilization parameter (e.g., temperature only). May be used to meet the internal CI recommendation.
Class 4
Multi-Variable Indicator
Reacts to two or more critical parameters of the sterilization cycle. May be used to meet the internal CI recommendation.
Class 5
Integrating Indicator
Reacts to all critical parameters (time, temperature, sterilizing agent). Used with a BI in PCD for implant loads. Cannot replace a BI.
Class 6
Emulating Indicator
Changes color when exposed to critical variables for a specific sterilization cycle. Within a PCD, may be used to monitor all sterilizer loads.

Guideline III — Biological indicators (BI)

🧫
BIs are the only sterilization monitoring device that provides a direct measure of the lethality of the sterilization process. They use microbes in the spore stage that are highly resistant to the type of sterilization being monitored.
🧫BI spore selection
  • Steam sterilization: Geobacillus stearothermophilus (formerly Bacillus stearothermophilus)
  • Ethylene oxide (EtO) sterilization: Bacillus atrophaeus
  • Some BIs contain the spore with an enzyme-based early-readout capability — allows faster results
  • BIs must be used per the manufacturer's written IFU, including storage, handling, use, and interpretation of incubation results
📅BI monitoring frequency
  • BIs used within PCDs (process challenge devices) for routine sterilizer monitoring
  • Required: at least weekly
  • Recommended: every day the sterilizer is used
  • PCD may be a user-assembled challenge test pack or a commercially available disposable test pack; assembled per ANSI/AAMI ST79 (steam) or ST41 (EtO)

Physical monitoring

🖥️Physical monitoring of sterilization machines
  • Physical monitors are the gauges or displays on the sterilization machine indicating pressure, temperature, and time
  • Data is recorded on a record chart or computer printout for each load
  • Physical monitoring is part of the overall quality assurance program — used alongside external CIs, internal CIs, and BIs
  • Specific types of internal CIs may help detect specific machine malfunctions: air leaks, improper temperature, or poor quality steam

Implants & Reference

Implant quarantine rules, emergency release protocol, positive BI recall procedure, and complete glossary for both urinary catheterization and monitoring sterility guidelines.

🚫
Implants must be quarantined until BI results are available. Releasing implants before BI results are known is unacceptable and is the exception, not the rule.

Implant loads — BI and CI requirements

🦴Every sterilization load containing implants
  • Must be monitored with a PCD containing a BI
  • The PCD must also contain a Class 5 integrating CI
  • Although Class 5 CIs have been correlated to BIs, they do not contain spores and cannot directly measure lethality — therefore, a Class 5 CI cannot be the sole means of verifying sterilizer efficacy; both BI and Class 5 CI are required
  • Implants must be quarantined until BI results are available and interpreted

Emergency release of implants

Guideline III · Emergency Release
When implants must be released before BI results are available
▼
⚠️
Emergency release is an exception, not a routine practice. If implants are being released routinely before BI results, the surgery department must take steps to resolve the root cause (e.g., purchasing additional instrument sets).
  • If implants must be released before BI results (e.g., emergency need for orthopedic screw and plate set): use of a Class 5 integrating CI provides additional information about critical parameters
  • Required documentation for emergency release:
    • The release of the implants must be documented
    • The results of the Class 5 integrating CI must be documented
    • BI results must be documented when available (as routinely performed)
    • Patient information must be included to trace the release and use of implants to the exact patient
  • The surgery department and CSPD must have written P&Ps defining emergency situations when implants are released prior to BI results — developed with the infection control officer, surgeon(s), and risk management
  • Annex L of ANSI/AAMI ST79:2010/A4:2013 provides examples of an implant log and an exception form

Positive BI results — recall protocol

🔴Positive BI result — required actions

A positive BI result initiates a recall of all items in the load processed in that specific sterilizer.

All items processed in that sterilizer since the last negative BI test must be considered non-sterile.

Surgery personnel assist in retrieving all affected items from sterile storage in the surgery department.

All retrieved items are returned to the CSPD for reprocessing and re-sterilization.
